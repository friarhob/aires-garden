It was [Tony Oreglia](https://www.tonycodes.com/garden/) who introduced me to the concept of a [digital garden](https://maggieappleton.com/garden-history), back when we worked together a few years ago. The visible part was a site of posts on technology, recipes, book recommendations, and poetry — but he explained to me what a garden ought to be, beyond what it appears to be.

I've been called a [polymath](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polymath) (my detractors would call me "that bloke with no focus", and they wouldn't be wrong either). Whatever you make of it, I've thrown myself into a great many things in this life (and I've already lived a fair stretch of years). I formally studied engineering, then philosophy, then marketing, then pedagogy, then psychoanalysis, and informally more than a few other subjects as well. I've worked as a programmer, as a data analyst/scientist, as a psychoanalyst, as a complementary therapist, as a musician, as a people manager, as a consultant, as a writer, as a copywriter, as a comic-book scriptwriter, and as a teacher/professor. As a teacher, in fact, I taught at undergrad / grad level: philosophy, statistics, finance, business administration, people management, theory of computation, software engineering, and mathematics — across courses in the mythical four great areas (STEM, Humanities, Social Sciences and Life Sciences — a taxonomy I've never managed to find any sense in, TBH, but which plenty of people use). A dear colleague used to say I was "a great Swiss army knife" whenever we headed into the dreaded faculty loading system.

And yet, every time I put content on the Internet, I was talking about specific aspects of specific subjects. I built blogs that have since gone extinct, on platforms that today only exist in the Web Archive (if you're lucky), about narrow topics — some that lasted three posts and some that lasted years. As I grow older, I lose more and more patience with social networks and the constant chase for presenting and broadcasting whoever it is we want other people to think we are. I have an absolute contempt for opinions (particularly my own) and for poorly-structured thoughts (though I avoid even debating with the people who hold them — disdaining ideas has nothing to do with disdaining people, by the way). Shedding more and more of the many certainties I once carried has been a gift I decided to give myself, with the privilege of years and a white beard.

So why build a digital garden? The truth is I'd like somewhere to keep my writing, and somewhere more centralised to create it. My friend Tony showed me that I didn't have a way, for instance, to share my poetry with a friend the way he had with me. I don't want fame; I don't want my photo anywhere on this site. I just want to leave ideas behind, and eventually rethink them and rewrite them and even delete them. If anyone wants to read along and offer complements and/or counterpoints, please feel free. But don't expect much engagement from my side.

It's entirely possible I'll rewrite this entry from scratch, by the way, or even delete it outright — I needed a placeholder to test the environment I'm building for all this. Until I do, here it is.

About me: it's not important. I'm not important.
